Austin Shaw

Hi, thank you for visiting my website. I am an engineer with 3+ years of experience working in product development and product quality within the outdoor industry. My passions are cooking, cycling, running, spending time outdoors, music, and programming.

Background

I was born in Charlotte, NC, and lived there until attending Appalachian State University in Boone, NC. In school I excelled at math but struggled to find an application of that skill that excited me. At Appalachian State, I took my first physics course and realized that this was exactly what I was looking for. I changed my major to applied physics shortly after that.

Education

Throughout my undergraduate degree I became interested in working with electronics and gravitated towards the courses that got me experience designing, building, and testing circuits. This lead to me applying for the graduate engineering physics degree offered at Appalachian State, which I was accepted into.

The engineering physics program was where I was first introduced to programming. Oddly enough the first programming 'language' that I ever learned was LabVIEW. I'm sure there are some pros and countless cons to starting with a graphical programming language such as LabVIEW, but I quickly moved on to other languages including assembly, C, C#, MATLAB, VHDL, and ladder logic for PLCs.

Team Sunergy

While finishing my undergraduate and degree and then also while working on my graduate degree at Appalachian State I was involved with the university's solar vehicle team, Team Sunergy. Team Sunergy was formed a few years before I joined, and had had success in previous years competing at the annual Formula Sun Grand Prix, a multi-day track race event, and the biennial American Solar Challenge, a multi-day point to point race on real roads with real cars. The team was split into subteams, business/operations, electrical, and mechanical. Naturally I joined the electrical team and began to learn about the system that powered the car.

My first year with the team, we travelled to Austin, TX to compete in the Formula Sun Grand Prix (FSGP) at the Circuit of the Americas Formula 1 track. That year we raced Apperion, our single-occupant solar vehicle whose body and solar cells had been donated to Appalachian State from Iowa State but whose electronics and suspension had been built and maintained by our team. After multiple long days on the track filled with ups and downs, we placed 2nd as we had completed the 2nd most amount of laps throughout the time on the track. There was no American Solar Challenge (ASC) this year, so our team returned to NC after a successful FSGP.

The following year I was nominated as the head of the electrical team on Team Sunergy, which was a large task as we had decided to ditch Apperion and to build a new solar vehicle from the ground up. The competitions were introducing a new category, for multi-occupant vehicles, and we wanted to be one of the first teams to enter this category, so in less than a year we built a two-person solar vehicle from nothing. I lead a team of students in the design and build of the electrical system, and personally designed and built the battery pack and battery management system.

The following Summer we brought our new car, ROSE, to the FSGP and ASC and placed 2nd and 1st respectively for the multi-occupant class.

Internship Experience

The Summer after finishing graduate school I accepted an internship offer with Borg Warner in Asheville, NC. This was a short, 6 week internship as this was the same Summer that I was racing ROSE. This was also the first internship I had accepted because I had sacrificed my previous Summers on working with Team Sunergy on the solar car.

During this internship it was my responsibility to automate a time consuming process of calibrating k-type thermocouples that were used to measure super high temperatures while testing turbochargers. This was to be achieved using LabVIEW using a queued message handler. The computer was to set the temperature of a calibration bath device using RS-232 and read the thermocouples' temperatures from a Fluke Black Stack using RS-422.

In my 6 weeks I tested, documented, and began implementing the commands to the calibration bath and the Fluke, and laid out the structure of queued message handler. I handed my work over to another intern as it was never planned for the project to be finished in 6 weeks.
